Which NER Tool Best Detects Person Names for Anonymization?

Anonymization Benchmark Brief — 50 European Court of Human Rights documents

What We Tested

We compared five tools for detecting person names in legal documents. The goal was to find which tool catches the most names (recall) with the fewest false alarms (precision), and how long each takes to run. We used the Text Anonymization Benchmark (TAB), a dataset of court documents where human experts labeled every person name. A detection was counted as correct if it overlapped with an expert-labeled name (e.g. "Dr. Johnson" and "Johnson", or "Joe Biden" and "Biden" count as an overlap.)

Key Findings

spaCy transformer is the clear winner. It caught 97% of person names with 89% precision, giving an F1 of 0.92. No other tool came close. At 30 seconds for 50 documents, the runtime is reasonable for production use.

Our current default (educonvokit) leaves a significant gap. With an F1 of 0.75, it misses roughly 1 in 5 person names and flags some false positives. Since spaCy trf runs in 30 seconds vs. 14 seconds, the speed difference is not meaningful enough to justify the accuracy tradeoff.

The generative LLM approach was not successful in this test, but may have potential. The local LLM (Qwen2.5-3B) scored lowest at F1 = 0.54 and took 15 minutes. However LLMs have been shown to be successful for some NER, so it could be that this specific configuration is the reason why the LLM performs poorly.

Recommendation: Switch from educonvokit to spaCy trf (en_core_web_trf) for production anonymization. It is substantially more accurate (F1 of 0.92 vs. 0.75) with only a modest increase in runtime (30s vs. 14s). The LLM approach may be worth revisiting.
Methodology note: All methods were evaluated on PERSON entities only. Scores are macro-averaged across 50 documents from the TAB dataset (Pillan et al., 2022), which contains European Court of Human Rights proceedings with names annotated by domain experts. This context is meaningfully different from classroom dialogues, thus results may differ on classroom datasets.
